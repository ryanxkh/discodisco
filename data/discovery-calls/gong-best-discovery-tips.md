# The 11 Best Discovery Call Tips

By Chris Orlob (Gong Labs research, based on analysis of recorded sales calls).

**These effective discovery call tips will help your team succeed:**

- Discovery is a process, not an event
- Match your questions to the buyer's journey
- Re-validate everything you uncover
- Phrase your questions to get long answers
- Say the three "magic" words
- Use "commercial-grade" listening
- Ask questions based on what your buyer said
- Ask questions only an expert could ask
- Ask the "sweet spot" number of questions
- Maintain a healthy talk-to-listen ratio
- Turn on your webcam

## Tip #1: Discovery is a process, not an event

Discovery calls aren't a STAGE during the sales cycle. Discovery is an ongoing process.

Your buyer's situation is in flux. If you do "set it and forget it" discovery, your deal will go off the rails right under your nose.

Bad salespeople treat discovery as a "check the box" event. They have a standard list of discovery questions. They ask them as if they're working their way through a checklist.

AVERAGE reps ask all their questions at the beginning of the call. They frontload discovery, as if completing a to-do list before launching into their pitch.

The top performers **spread their discovery questions evenly** across their sales calls. To them, **discovery is an ongoing process**, not an event.

> _The best discovery calls I hear don't sound like discovery._

## Tip #2: Match your questions to the buyer's journey

Your first few discovery questions should respect your prospect's stage in the buying journey.

**Example:** If you learn that your buyer is ACTIVELY evaluating products like yours, **don't** start with this kind of question:

_What's your biggest strategic priority for this year?_

That's a _diagnostic_ question. Its purpose is to uncover a pain point, which is appropriate for early stages of the buyer journey.

But your buyer is beyond that stage. They're _evaluating products._ So if you start with this question, you'll irritate them.

Don't start with it. Start with sales discovery questions that respect the buyer's journey stage.

In this case, your buyer is in the _evaluation stage_. So ask them THIS question:

_What do you hope to achieve by implementing a solution?_

This question matches the buyer's stage. It's frictionless. **It's what the buyer _wants_ to talk about.**

Once (and only once) you've asked that, can you go back to the diagnostic question:

- **You:** What are you hoping to achieve by implementing a solution?
- **Buyer:** We're looking to increase sales skills.
- **You:** Good to know. What's causing you to prioritize that as an initiative?
- **Buyer:** Our revenue target has increased, but our marketing budget has not. We've got to do more with less. So, better selling skills is a big part of that answer.

The second discovery question goes BACK to the beginning stage of the buyer's journey (pain), but only once your first discovery question acknowledges that the buyer is already well into their journey. Meet them where they stand.

## Tip #3: Re-validate everything you uncover

Never assume that what you learned at the beginning of the sales process remains true.

Priorities change. Buyers' needs are transient.

Start every sales meeting with this question:

> _"What's changed since the last time we talked?"_

## Tip #4: Phrase your questions to get LONG answers

Successful salespeople get LONG answers to their questions. **There's a strong link between buyers' response lengths and closed deals.**

Here's a discovery call tip to help you do that: **SIGNAL to your buyer that you want a long response. Do that by phrasing your questions the right way.**

Start with your basic _who, what, when, where, why,_ or _how_ question. Then frame it with phrases like:

- "Can you help me understand…"
- "Can you walk me through…"
- "Talk to me about…"

For example:

_"What's your biggest challenge?"_ becomes _"Can you help me understand your biggest challenge?"_

This phrasing signals that you want your buyer to answer _in depth_. You'll get richer answers that help you close deals.

## Tip #5: Say these three "magic" words

If you tried the last tip, you've got your buyer _talking._

**Here's how to keep them talking so you can learn even more.**

When they've finished responding to your discovery question:

- Repeat the main 1-3 words your customer used in their response.
- Do it with an upward voice tone, like you're asking a question.

Like this:

- **Buyer**: _We're losing deals to competitors. Our product is strong, but our reps back peddle when customers ask how we're different._
- **You**: _Back peddle?_
- **Buyer**: _Yes. They don't respond with a crisp, strong answer. They stumble. And their answers are inconsistent._
- **You**: _Inconsistent?_
- **Buyer**: _Yeah, that's another problem we have. I have 70 sales reps running 70 different sales processes…_

This sales technique is called _mirroring_ (popularized by Chris Voss). When you do it right, you open up the floodgates.

**The key is to repeat your _buyers' own words_ back to them.** Not your words. And then _stop talking._

## Tip #6: Use "commercial-grade" listening

Your next step is to _label_ your buyer's emotions.

**When you notice your buyer getting emotional, _label_ that emotion.** Use sentence starters like "It sounds like…" or "It seems like…" followed by an accurate label of the emotion your buyer expressed.

This is another Chris Voss technique. An example:

- **Buyer**: _It's a TON of work to onboard new sales reps. Creating the training material is bringing our enablement department to its knees._
- **You**: _It sounds like you feel overwhelmed every time a new onboarding class starts._
- **Buyer**: _I do! I feel a sense of dread every Sunday night before the work week starts._

If you labeled their emotion right, you made your buyer feel _deeply_ understood.

The key is to shut your mouth after you've voiced your label. Let your buyer talk uninterrupted.

## Tip #7: Ask questions based on what your buyer said

**Don't start a new thread with every question.**

Go deep. Base your next question on what your buyer _just told you_.

Your discovery call questions should feel spontaneous. When you DON'T take this approach, it seems like you're sticking to an annoying line of questioning. It doesn't feel like a natural conversation.

Instead, ask your questions as a _follow-up_ to what your customer said:

- Sales rep: _Can you help me understand where you lose deals most often in your sales process?_
- Buyer: _After they see a product demo. The buyer gets excited. But then they have the daunting task of getting their VP scheduled for a sales call with us. Weeks go by. We follow up. And then never hear back._
- Sales rep: _Interesting. What would you need to change in order to prevent that from happening?_

**Your second question shouldn't start a new thread.** It should go deeper into an issue the buyer raised.

## Tip #8: Ask questions only an expert could ask

The best questions you can ask are ones that only an _expert_ would ask. **They're questions that make your buyer _think_ rather than just recite information.** They signal to your prospective buyer that you're _a competent professional they can trust._

Example. When I was a sales rep, I often asked this question:

> _"Can you walk me through your sales process, from when you first generate a new lead, through to a closed deal?"_

The wording matters. It signals a level of seriousness and expertise. Compare with the trite, _"What's your sales process?"_

Even though they ask the same thing, only one demonstrates expertise.

## Tip #9: Ask the "sweet spot" number of questions

There's a sweet spot for how many discovery questions you should ask during a sales call: **between 11 and 14**.

If you ask too many sales discovery questions, the conversation will feel like an interrogation. Too few, and you won't unearth enough pain points.

**HOWEVER … those numbers don't apply when you're selling to a C-Suite executive.** Their win rates drop after asking a small handful of questions.

In fact, successful meetings with C-suite executives have only **four questions on average**. Unsuccessful meetings have eight.

That's because senior executives have "discovery fatigue." By the time they have a meeting with you, they've likely been through 2 or 3 discovery calls with other sellers. Answering all those questions feels _taxing._

## Tip #10: Maintain a healthy talk-to-listen ratio

The best salespeople talk for **46% of their sales calls** (on average). They leave 54% of the talking time to the customer.

Maintaining a healthy talk-to-listen ratio is so obvious that it should be the first tip. Why isn't it? **Because it's a byproduct of doing everything else right.**

Talking for 46% of a sales call isn't going to magically help you close deals. But if you follow the _other_ tips in this post, you'll _end up_ with this ratio (more or less).

At the very least, stay in the recommended RANGE. Don't go ABOVE 65%, which is where you start to lose deals.

## Tip #11: Turn on your webcam

Why is this a DISCOVERY call tip? Because it's _more important_ to turn on your webcam during discovery calls than during other types of calls.

Other types of calls already have a visual backdrop:

- Demo calls have a product demo
- Presentations have a visual slide deck

Discovery calls are _mano y mano_: you and the buyer.

**If you _don't_ have your webcam on, your buyer has nothing to look at.** And they're more likely to grow tired of your questions (or act rudely).

**Closed deals involve using webcams 41% _more_ often than lost deals.**
